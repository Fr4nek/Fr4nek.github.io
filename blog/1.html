How Far Can We Go to Make an iPhone Truly Secure?

I like this idea—or rather, this thought experiment: how do you create a device that is virtually invisible in a world of digital surveillance?

Taped-over Cameras

This is the most pragmatic step, considering how common it is to see tape on laptop webcams, especially in the security industry. There is a certain hypocrisy in the fact that the same people leave their phone lenses uncovered—devices that accompany us not just in the office, but often in our most intimate moments.

I did this in the simplest way possible: I cut small circles out of black electrical tape and precisely applied them to the lenses. Secure doesn’t have to mean ugly. Think of it as camouflage—a phone with clearly visible, sloppily taped cameras might raise unnecessary suspicion.

No Apple ID Account

Moving on to the configuration of a "fresh" device: Apple, of course, asks you to sign in to an Apple ID. While they are one of the few companies offering advanced data encryption, we are adopting the mindset of someone tracked by Interpol. We don’t want to risk linking any activity to our phone number, which is required to create an account. We remain offline within the ecosystem.

Connectivity and Further Configuration

Unless we have access to trusted public Wi-Fi, we skip the network connectivity step. A home router has its own IP and logs. Even if we connect to it only for a moment, that fact will be recorded, and data sent from a home IP could link the device's activity to our physical location.

Naturally, we also skip telemetry settings and Apple Pay configuration. Every card payment leaves a massive digital footprint, and that is the last thing we want.

One interesting tactic is choosing English as the system language. In the event the phone is lost or seized, it becomes harder to determine our nationality at first glance. When prompted for a screen lock (Touch ID in my case), we set a robust, at least 6-digit passcode.

Lockdown Mode – The Final Bastion

The real game-changer in our scenario is Lockdown Mode. This feature is designed for individuals who might be targeted by the most sophisticated state-sponsored cyberattacks. By activating it, we turn the smartphone into a digital fortress.

Installing Applications

Unfortunately, to install any application on an iPhone, you must be signed in to an Apple ID. In our scenario, we handle this by creating a completely new, "clean" account that is not linked to our real identity. We sign in with it only within the App Store app (rather than the entire system). Immediately after installing the necessary tools, we sign out, leaving the device in a state of maximum isolation.

Crucial Note on Verification: Apple typically requires a phone number for two-factor authentication (2FA) when creating a new account. To avoid undermining our anonymity efforts, using a personal phone number is out of the question. The solution is to use an anonymous "prepaid" SIM card purchased with cash or a one-time "virtual burner number" service. This SIM should be used only once to activate the account and then either physically destroyed or securely stored away.

Selected Applications:

Signal: A widely recognized, open-source messenger featuring end-to-end encryption. It is the gold standard for secure communication.

Organic Maps: An open-source alternative to Google or Apple Maps. It doesn't connect to servers to track the user and allows for the use of full-country maps offline.

Proton Mail: The most popular encrypted email service, providing end-to-end encryption to ensure the privacy of your correspondence.

A Solid VPN (e.g., NordVPN or Proton VPN): An essential tool for masking your IP address and encrypting network traffic, especially when you have to use external access points.

Further System Settings Adjustments

Once I reach the home screen, I immediately head to the settings. The iPhone has several excellent, though underused, security features:

Erase Data: Once enabled, all data on the phone will be wiped after 10 failed passcode attempts. If someone tries to brute-force your code to uncover your secrets, everything will vanish forever.

Siri & Search: This is a crucial point. I disable everything: "Listen for 'Hey Siri'", "Press Side Button for Siri," and "Allow Siri When Locked." We don't want the phone constantly listening for a command, nor do we want the voice assistant to have access to any information without passcode authorization.

Allow Access When Locked: I uncheck every single option here. This ensures that anyone who gets hold of the phone cannot turn on Airplane Mode, read notifications, or control home accessories without entering the passcode.
